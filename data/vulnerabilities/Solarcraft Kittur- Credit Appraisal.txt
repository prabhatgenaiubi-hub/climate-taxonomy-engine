brief rating rationales: as under: 
key rating drivers & detailed description 
improvement in operational performance: power generation of the underperforming projects (barring 
agsepl) improved from fy23 post the rectification of cable faults, replacement of certain modules and 
resolution of the inverter tripping issues with ahsepl achieving a plant load factor of 25.12% during the 
trailing 12 months ended december 2023 (fy23: 24.34%; fy22: 20.64%). barring agsepl, all other 
projects had performed lower than p90 estimates since their commercial operations date till fy22. the 
agency expects the generation levels to be stable and any material underperformance due to legacy or 
unresolved issues will be a key rating monitorable. 
firm off-take agreement; agsepl, akisepl and akusepl have power purchase agreements (ppas) with 
bangalore electricity supply company limited, whereas asaspl and ahsepl have ppas with hubli
electricity supply company limited (debt rated at 'ind a'/stable). the ppas of all the projects are 
valid for 25 years from the effective date (date on which the tariff was approved). the ppas stipulate 
power generation at a maximum capacity utilisation factor of 26% and a minimum capacity utilisation 
factor of 14%. the presence of long-term ppas assures cash flows to the project and mitigates revenue 
risk to a large extent. 
moderate sponsor profile: bepl acquired agsepl, akisepl, akusepl, ahsepl and asaspl from acme 
group in january 2024. actis, a global investor in sustainable infrastructure, has committed to invest up 
to usd800 million in bepl through actis' energy 5 fund. bepl, founded in november 2021, had acquired 
an operational solar portfolio of 724mw and about 1.2gw of solar, wind and hybrid projects under 
various stages of implementation. 
kerc-approved levelised tariff for compensation towards safeguard duty: karnataka electricity 
reguulatory commission (kerc) had approved a levelised tariff for four projects (barring akusepl) in 
march 2023, which will be paid for a 25-year period in the form of annuity on the minimum units of 
generation as stipulated in the ppa. with respect to akusepl, a kerc order dated 11 june 2021 has 
dismissed the petition filed by the project related to compensation on safeguard duty. as per the 
management, the company has filed a review petition and the next hearing is due to be conducted. 
liquidity indicator - adequate: the projects together had about inr48.09 million, largely in the form of 
free cash as of 31 january 2024. the liquidity, is estimated to cover about 1.4 months of annual debt 
servicing obligations of the entities in the obco structure. bepl has mentioned to the agency that its 
plans to replenish the dsra for these projects either in the form of a bank guarantee or cash. the 
absence of a dsra is a rating constraint. although the projects have been receiving payments within 90 
days from their respective counterparties, any extension in the receivables, would require financial 
support from the sponsor. 
moderate debt structure: the final project cost of all the five projects together is inr4,697 million, 
funded in a debt:equity ratio of about 68:32. the initial sanctioned funding debt:equity ratio was 75:25. 
the earlier sponsor, as per the terms of the financing documents, had infused additional funds, more 
than the committed equity to cover the cost overruns related to the safeguard duty. the debt is

crisil industry research solar power-april 2024 
demand in the solar energy sector is primarily driven by central and state solar policies, as well as the 
renewable purchase obligation (rpo) targets set by individual states. the rpo mandates state-owned 
and private distribution companies (discoms), open access entities, and captive users to procure a fixed 
percentage of their power from renewable sources. for states with limited solar potential, they have 
the option of using renewable energy certificates (recs) to meet their rpo obligations. the overall 
compliance with solar renewable purchase obligations (rpos) is estimated to be approximately 91 
percent in 2022-23, following significant revisions by the ministry of power. however, this achievement 
is largely attributed to a few key states with lower rpo targets. karnataka, telangana and andhra 
pradesh have successfully met their solar rpo targets, accounting for around 22 percent of the total 
installed capacity as of march 2024. in addition to these state-level initiatives, the central government's 
national solar mission, cop-26 target of achieving 50% non-fossil fuel energy penetration by 2030, 
promotion of green hydrogen, green energy open access rules, and fiscal incentives such as the 
accelerated depreciation scheme (providing 40 percent depreciation in the first year of operations) and 
the ministry of new and renewable energy's rooftop solar subsidy scheme will further stimulate the 
demand for solar power. it is anticipated that capacity additions will reach 125-130 gw between fy25 
and fy29, with further upside of 34.38 gw possible on account of green hydrogen additions. to sustain 
this growth in solar capacity, it is essential to enforce rpos, promote rooftop solar adoption, establish 
consistent and sustainable policies across states, secure low-cost financing, and ensure the availability 
of suitable land and grid infrastructure. although the potential for solar power is substantial, the solar 
sector remains highly competitive, with the adoption of li bidding (reverse bidding) under the national 
solar mission and various state policies. furthermore, the sector has witnessed increased participation 
from global independent power producers (ipps) and large indian conglomerates. 
demand-supply 
demand in the solar energy sector is primarily driven by central and state solar policies, as well as the 
renewable purchase obligation (rpo) targets set by individual states. the rpo mandates that both 
state-owned and private distribution companies (discoms), open access entities, and captive users 
procure a fixed percentage of their power requirement from renewable energy sources. for states with 
insufficient solar potential, they can utilize the renewable energy certificate (rec) route to fulfill their 
rpo obligations. the overall compliance with solar renewable purchase obligations (rpos) was 
estimated to be approximately 90-95 percent in 2022-23. however, karnataka, telangana, and andhra 
pradesh were among the few states that met their solar rpo targets, collectively accounting for

government policies 
overall, government policy has been favorable to the sector. solar power generators enjoy a-40 percent 
accelerated depreciation benefit. the newly launched surya yojana in 2024 has been planned to boost 
the residential offtake of solar rooftop. subsidy levels have increased from rs 18,000 to rs 30,000 on a 
per kw basis for plant size up to 2 kw. for plant size of 3 kw, maximum subsidy available is rs 78,000 in 
total, with this remaining as a cap for project sizes larger than 3 kw. along with this, free electricity 
upto 300 units per month per household has been announced. this essentially means, the 66% jump in 
subsidy support has been directed mainly to the smaller sized rooftop projects, which would comprise 
the majority of solar rooftop potential in india and would also face affordability concerns the most due 
to small sizing. to further promote open-access installations, the government recently unveiled the 
2022 green energy open-access rules. these rules address key issues in the segment, including high 
levies, the absence of banking provisions, and procedural variability. they not only benefit private 
consumers seeking private power transactions but also provide a mechanism for discoms to directly sell 
green power to consumers. in 2023, the government amended the definition of "entity." the new 
definition includes entities with contracted demand/sanctioned loads of 100 kw or more through 
multiple connections aggregating 100 kw or more within the same electricity division of a discom 
licensee, excluding captive consumers. this amendment benefits sectors like telecom, which typically 
have low sanctioned loads of 10 kw per site. under the previous rules, each site was treated as a 
separate entity, resulting in operational costs. . with over 7 lakh sites in india, this amendment 
encourages the adoption of renewable energy and cost savings. central and state governments have 
provided substantial support. the national solar mission includes a payment security mechanism to 
protect developers and lenders in case of discom defaults. the union budget for 2023-24 increased 
budgetary allocation by 48 percent over the revised estimate of fy23 to rs 73.27 billion for solar power 
grid, off-grid, and kusum projects. amidst the covid pandemic, mnre extended support to developers 
by granting a total extension of 7.5 months for all solar projects. in march 2022, mnre extended the 
timeline for the mandatory use of modules from the approved list of models and manufacturers (almm) 
for open access/net-metering projects from april 1, 2021, to october 1, 2022, providing relief to the 
open access and solar rooftop segments. additionally, the ministry of power allowed the bundling of 
thermal power and renewable energy within existing power purchase agreements (ppas), aiding in the 
goal of achieving panchamrut targets and shifting the burden from discoms to generators for tying up 
renewable energy. mnre has made several amendments to bidding guidelines and simplified procedures 
to encourage faster adoption of solar power. in september 2022, mnre's move to consider basic 
customs duty (bcd) on the import of cells and modules as a 'change in law' event could lead to a tariff 
increase for some projects, offering partial relief. infrastructural challenges have impacted execution 
momentum in solar intermittently, though the problem is more acute in wind. resolution of 
infrastructural issues is critical, where -53% of pipeline seci projects for which ppa has been signed are 
delayed due to transmission and infrastructural issues. along with this, land issues remains another 
crucial area since 3.3 lakh acres of land has been utilized for solar projects and additional capacity of 
21 lakh acres is still required to meet government targets by 2032. additionally, the ministry of power 
announced that the interstate transmission system (ists) waiver on solar projects commissioned after 
june 2025 will be phased out gradually until june 30, 2028, potentially impacting the final cost of solar 
power to utilities and customers. in summary, while india's solar power sector has thrived on, supportive 
policies and initiatives, policy consistency across states and also between the solar energy development 
and solar supply chain segments is needed along with a focus on quickly ramping, up associated 
infrastructural resources.

extent of competition 
while the central government (nsm phase ii) and various state governments are expected to allocate 
significant capacities, we anticipate that stiff competition will persist. the entry of globally established 
players with access to cheap capital and economies of scale led to aggressive bidding in late fiscal 2021 
and the first half of ,fiscal 2022. furthermore, with the participation of large conglomerates and 
well established global solar companies, coupled with low input costs and fund availability, competition 
intensified.however, significant consolidation activity in the sector over the past 1-2 years has limited 
competition to a pool of 10-15 players, with not all participating in each auction. this rationalized the 
aggressiveness in bidding to some extent, as players recognized the implementation risks associated 
with the tariffs they bid for. additionally, cost pressures in fiscal 2023 resulted in an upward trend in 
solar tariffs, reaching rs 2.79 per unit, with developers factoring in price volatility risk due to supply 
shortages. the anticipated cooling of commodity prices in fiscal 2024 has led to only a marginal decline 
in bid tariffs to rs 2.61/unit. we expect tariffs to remain in the same range of rs. 2.5-2.6 per unit in 
fiscal 2025 as developers tread cautiously in a changing environment for solar module supply.
